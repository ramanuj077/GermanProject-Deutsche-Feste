About Oktoberfest

Discover the world's most famous folk festival

Oktoberfest is the world's largest Volksfest (beer festival and travelling funfair). Held annually in Munich, Bavaria, Germany, it is a 16- to 18-day folk festival running from mid- or late-September to the first weekend in October, with more than 6 million people from around the world attending the event every year.

Location & Dates

Theresienwiese, Munich • Late September to First Sunday in October

The festival grounds, known as "Theresienwiese" or "Wiesn" for short, cover 420,000 square meters in central Munich. The 2023 festival runs from September 16th to October 3rd.

Where Oktoberfest Begins

Schottenhamel

Capacity: 10,000 Spaten Brewery

The Schottenhamel tent is where the official tapping of the first keg takes place, marking the start of Oktoberfest. It's particularly popular with younger crowds and students, featuring lively music and a festive atmosphere.

International Favorite

Hofbräu Festzelt

Capacity: 6,900 Hofbräu Brewery

Known as the most international tent, Hofbräu Festzelt is particularly popular with visitors from Australia, the US, and the UK. It features a lively "Ein Prosit" singalong and traditional Bavarian music mixed with international hits.

Munich's Favorite

Augustiner-Festhalle

Capacity: 6,000 Augustiner Brewery

Considered by many locals to serve the best beer at Oktoberfest, the Augustiner tent maintains a more traditional, family-friendly atmosphere. It's known for serving beer from wooden barrels rather than stainless steel tanks.

Oktoberfest History

Over 200 years of Bavarian tradition

The First Oktoberfest

The first Oktoberfest was held in 1810 to celebrate the marriage of Crown Prince Ludwig (later King Ludwig I) to Princess Therese of Saxony-Hildburghausen. The citizens of Munich were invited to attend the festivities held on the fields in front of the city gates, named Theresienwiese ("Theresa's fields") in honor of the bride.

1810

Agricultural Fair Addition

In 1811, an agricultural show was added to promote Bavarian agriculture. This tradition continues today with the Central Agricultural Festival taking place every three years during Oktoberfest, featuring livestock competitions and farming exhibitions.

1811

Beer Tents Introduced

Small beer stands were introduced in 1818, which gradually grew into the massive beer halls we know today. The first carousel and swings were also set up, marking the beginning of the amusement park aspect of the festival.

1818

Modern Oktoberfest Born

In the late 19th century, Oktoberfest transformed into the modern festival we recognize today. Electric lighting illuminated the festival in 1880, and the first beer was served in glass mugs in 1892. The statue of Bavaria was completed in 1850, becoming an iconic symbol of the festival.

Plan Your Trip

Essential information for your Oktoberfest visit

Getting There

Munich Airport (MUC) is well-connected internationally. From the airport, take the S-Bahn (S1 or S8) to the city center. The festival grounds are easily accessible by public transportation (U-Bahn U4/U5 to Theresienwiese).

Accommodation

Book hotels months in advance as they fill up quickly. Consider staying in neighborhoods slightly outside the city center for better availability and prices. Alternative options include Airbnb, hostels, or nearby towns with train access.

Tickets & Reservations

Entry to Oktoberfest is free, but reservations for beer tents are essential, especially for weekends and evenings. Reservations open in spring through individual tent websites. Some tables may be available for walk-ins on weekdays.

Budget Planning

Budget approximately €12-€15 per liter of beer, €15-€25 for main dishes, and €5-€10 for snacks. Bring cash as many vendors don't accept cards. Consider purchasing a mass (liter) mug as a souvenir (€10-€20 deposit).

Oktoberfest Food & Drink

Traditional Bavarian cuisine and specialties

Oktoberfest Beer

Specially brewed Märzen beer with an alcohol content of 6-7%. Only six Munich breweries are allowed to serve beer at Oktoberfest: Augustiner, Hacker-Pschorr, Hofbräu, Löwenbräu, Paulaner, and Spaten. Served in one-liter mugs called "mass".

Hendl (Roast Chicken)

Half a roast chicken, typically seasoned with salt and sometimes herbs. Over 500,000 chickens are consumed each year at Oktoberfest. Crispy skin and juicy meat make this a festival favorite, often served with a pretzel or potato salad.

Schweinshaxe

Pork knuckle roasted until the skin is crispy and the meat is tender. A Bavarian specialty that's particularly popular at Oktoberfest. Typically served with sauerkraut, dumplings, and mustard. A hearty meal perfect with beer.

Brezen (Pretzels)

Large, soft Bavarian pretzels with a chewy interior and crispy, salted exterior. Often served with Obatzda, a spicy cheese spread, or sweet mustard. The perfect beer accompaniment, with over 130,000 pretzels consumed annually.

Oktoberfest Etiquette Guide

Do's and Don'ts for the perfect Wiesn experience

Do's

  • Make reservations for beer tents well in advance, especially for weekends
  • Wear traditional Tracht (Dirndl for women, Lederhosen for men) if possible
  • Make eye contact when toasting (saying "Prost!")
  • Tip your server (round up or add 5-10%)
  • Use public transportation to get to the festival grounds
  • Try traditional Bavarian foods like Hendl and Brezen
  • Learn a few basic German phrases like "Prost" and "Danke"

Don'ts

  • Forget to bring cash - many vendors don't accept cards
  • Steal beer mugs - it's illegal and carries heavy fines
  • Stand on the benches - only step on them when toasting
  • Assume you'll get a table without a reservation, especially on weekends
  • Forget to validate your public transportation ticket before boarding
  • Bring large bags or backpacks - security checks are strict
  • Overindulge - pace yourself with the strong beer and plenty of food

Essential German Phrases for Oktoberfest

Prost! (Cheers!) • Ein Maß, bitte. (One liter of beer, please.) • O'zapft is! (It's tapped! - traditional opening phrase) • Hendl (Chicken) • Brezn (Pretzel) • Zahlen, bitte. (Check, please.) • Wo ist die Toilette? (Where is the toilet?)
