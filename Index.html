World's Largest Volksfest

Oktoberfest

Munich, Bavaria Late September - Early October

The world's largest folk festival, celebrating Bavarian culture with beer, food, and traditional music in Munich's Theresienwiese. Attracting over 6 million visitors annually, this 16-18 day festival features 14 large tents and 20 small tents serving specially brewed Oktoberfest beer.

German Christmas Markets

Experience the Magic of Weihnachtsmärkte

German Christmas markets (Weihnachtsmärkte) are a beloved tradition dating back to the Late Middle Ages. Starting in late November and running until Christmas Eve, these markets transform town squares into winter wonderlands filled with festive lights, aromas of mulled wine and roasted almonds, and the sound of Christmas carols.

Famous Christmas Markets

Nuremberg Christkindlesmarkt: One of Germany's oldest and most famous Christmas markets, dating back to 1628. The market is opened by the Christkind (Christmas Angel) and features over 180 stalls selling traditional crafts, Nuremberg sausages, and gingerbread.

Dresden Striezelmarkt: Germany's oldest documented Christmas market, first held in 1434. Named after the Christmas stollen (Striezel), this market features a massive 14-meter high Christmas pyramid and focuses on traditional crafts from the Ore Mountains.

Cologne Cathedral Market: Set against the stunning backdrop of Cologne Cathedral, this market offers a magical atmosphere with its impressive Christmas tree, numerous craft stalls, and variety of regional specialties.

Traditional & Folk Festivals

Celebrating Germany's rich cultural heritage

World's Largest Wine Festival

Bad Dürkheim Wurstmarkt

Bad Dürkheim, Rhineland-Palatinate September

Despite its name meaning "Sausage Market," this is actually the world's largest wine festival. Dating back to 1417, it attracts over 600,000 visitors annually to sample wines from the Palatinate region. The festival features massive wine barrels, traditional food, and live music in a vibrant atmosphere.

Explore Wurstmarkt

Fifth Season Celebrations

Cologne Karneval

Cologne, North Rhine-Westphalia November - February

Germany's most famous carnival celebration, known as the "fifth season," culminating in massive parades on Rose Monday before Ash Wednesday. With origins dating to the Middle Ages, this festival features elaborate costumes, satirical performances, and the traditional greeting "Kölle Alaaf!" (Cologne above all!).

Discover Karneval

Marksmen's Festival Tradition

Hannover Schützenfest

Hannover, Lower Saxony July

The world's largest marksmen's festival, attracting over 1 million visitors annually. This tradition dates back to 1529 and features a grand parade with over 10,000 participants, fairground attractions, and the election of the Schützenkönig (Marksmen's King). The festival celebrates local marksmanship guilds and community spirit.

Music & Cultural Festivals

From classical to contemporary celebrations

Heavy Metal Pilgrimage

Wacken Open Air

Wacken, Schleswig-Holstein August

The world's largest heavy metal festival, attracting over 75,000 metalheads from around the globe. Since 1990, this small village transforms into a metal paradise each summer, featuring hundreds of bands across multiple stages. The festival is known for its incredible atmosphere, elaborate stages, and the famous "rain or shine" spirit.

Wagner's Musical Legacy

Bayreuth Festival

Bayreuth, Bavaria July - August

A prestigious music festival dedicated exclusively to the works of Richard Wagner. Held since 1876 in the purpose-built Bayreuth Festspielhaus, this festival attracts opera enthusiasts from around the world. The waiting list for tickets can be up to 10 years, making it one of the most exclusive cultural events in Germany.

International Film Celebration

Berlin International Film Festival

Berlin February

One of the world's leading film festivals, attracting over 400,000 attendees annually. Founded in 1951, the Berlinale showcases around 400 films each year and awards the prestigious Golden Bear. The festival combines glamorous red carpet events with a commitment to artistic cinema and political discourse.

Festival Food & Drink

Essential German festival cuisine

Bratwurst

Grilled pork sausage served in a fresh roll with mustard. A festival staple found everywhere. Regional variations include Nürnberger Rostbratwurst (small, thin sausages) and Thüringer Rostbratwurst (longer, seasoned with marjoram).

Currywurst

Sliced sausage with a tangy tomato-curry sauce, especially popular in Berlin. Invented in 1949 by Herta Heuwer in Berlin, this dish is typically served with fries or a bread roll and has become a German fast food icon.

Glühwein

Hot mulled wine with spices, the essential drink at German Christmas markets. Made with red wine, cinnamon sticks, cloves, star anise, citrus, and sugar. Non-alcoholic versions (Kinderpunsch) are available for children and non-drinkers.

Lebkuchen

Traditional German gingerbread, often heart-shaped and decorated with icing. Nuremberg is famous for its Elisenlebkuchen, a premium variety made with minimal flour and plenty of nuts. These spiced cookies have been enjoyed since the 13th century.

Festival Etiquette Guide

Do's and Don'ts for enjoying German festivals

Do's

  • Make eye contact when toasting (Prost!) - it's considered rude not to
  • Wear traditional Tracht (Dirndl/Lederhosen) properly if you choose to
  • Tip service staff (round up to nearest euro or 5-10% for good service)
  • Use public transportation to get to festivals - it's efficient and eco-friendly
  • Learn a few basic German phrases - locals appreciate the effort
  • Reserve tables in beer tents well in advance for popular festivals
  • Try regional specialties and drinks for an authentic experience

Don'ts

  • Forget to bring cash (many vendors, especially at markets, are cash-only)
  • Be too loud or unruly outside the festival tents - respect local residents
  • Wait for the check at the bar (servers will come to you when you're ready)
  • Forget to book accommodation well in advance for major festivals
  • Leave valuables unattended in crowded areas
  • Assume everyone speaks English - learn key phrases
  • Forget to return your glassware for the deposit (Pfand) at beer tents

Essential German Phrases

Prost! (Cheers!) • Ein Maß, bitte. (One liter of beer, please.) • Wie viel kostet das? (How much does that cost?) • Entschuldigung. (Excuse me/Sorry.) • Danke. (Thank you.) • Zahlen, bitte. (Check, please.) • Wo ist die Toilette? (Where is the toilet?)
